The window you see through

Stripes that get finer left-to-right and fainter bottom-to-top. The arch where they vanish is not in the image — it is the literal shape of your vision. Lean back; it moves.

Every column has the same stripes; every row has the same contrast. If you were a camera, the boundary between visible and invisible would be a straight horizontal line — a fixed contrast threshold. It isn't. You see an inverted arch: stripes too fine or too coarse disappear even at high contrast, while mid-frequencies stay visible far up into the faint zone. That arch is your contrast sensitivity function, peaking near where a face's features sit. Step back and the whole arch slides right. You are not seeing the chart — you are seeing the limits of the instrument that sees it.
