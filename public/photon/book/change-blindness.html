The change you can't catch · change blindness

One thing in this scene keeps changing — appearing, vanishing, or shifting colour — over and over. With the brief blank flicker between each version, you will struggle to spot it for a shockingly long time. Tap when you think you've found it (it'll be marked). Then turn the flicker off and watch how it screams at you.

With the image present continuously, a change produces a motion transient — a flash of "something moved here" — and your eye is yanked straight to it. The brief blank wipes that transient out: when the scene returns, every object has changed in the sense that it just blinked off and on, so there is no local signal pointing at the one that's truly different. To find it you must compare a remembered image to the present one, object by object — and it turns out you remember far less of a scene than it feels like you do. The vivid, detailed world in your head is largely an illusion of availability: detail is rendered where you attend, and almost nowhere else. You do not hold a picture of the world — you re-fetch it, on demand, and trust it's all there.
