One image, many worlds · vision as the inverse problem

Drag the object nearer or farther along the line of sight. As it moves, it resizes to stay inside the same cone of light — so the picture on the retina never changes. The eye is handed one image; the object that caused it could be anywhere on that ray.

The world is three-dimensional; the retina is a two-dimensional surface. Projecting 3D onto 2D throws depth away, and once depth is gone, infinitely many scenes could have produced the very same image — a small near object and a large far one are indistinguishable on the retina. The image is the effect; the scene is the cause; recovering a cause from its effect is an inverse problem, and it has no unique solution. Yet you see one definite world, instantly. Your visual system is not reading depth off the retina — it isn't there to read. It is inferring the most likely cause, using built-in assumptions about how the world usually is. Vision is not recording. It is the best-supported guess about what could have cast this image.
