One image, many worlds · vision as the inverse problem

From a single fixed viewpoint — the eye — these three objects cast the same circle on your retina. The retinal image alone cannot tell them apart. Move your head, and the one image splits into three different worlds.

This is the founding problem of vision, and the spine of the whole book. The world is three-dimensional; the retinal image is two-dimensional. Collapsing 3D to 2D throws away depth, and infinitely many scenes project to any given image — a small near sphere, a large far one, a tilted disc, all the same circle. The image is the effect; the scene is the cause; and recovering a cause from its effect is, mathematically, an inverse problem with no unique solution. Yet you see one definite world, instantly and without effort. Your visual system is not reading the answer off the retina — there is no answer there to read. It is guessing, brilliantly, using assumptions about how the world usually is. Vision is not recording. It is the best-supported guess about what could have caused this image.
