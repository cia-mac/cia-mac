One image, many worlds · vision as the inverse problem

Top-left is everything the eye actually receives: one flat image. The scene that caused it is drawn to the right. Drag the ball along the line of sight — it changes size and distance to stay inside the same rays, and the image in the eye never changes. Then ask the eye's question in reverse.

There are two directions here, and they are not equally easy. Forward — from a known scene to its image — has exactly one answer: physics projects the 3D world onto the 2D retina, and the picture is determined. Inverse — from the image back to the scene — has no unique answer at all. Flattening 3D to 2D destroys depth, and once depth is gone a near small ball and a far large one are the same picture. The retina hands the brain the image and nothing else; the world that made it is genuinely ambiguous. So the single, confident world you see is not measured — it is the visual system's best inference, leaning on assumptions about how things usually are. Vision is not recording. It is solving an unsolvable problem, well enough to act.
