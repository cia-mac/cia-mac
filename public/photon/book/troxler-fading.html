The world dissolves · Troxler fading

Stare at the centre cross and hold absolutely still. Within ten or twenty seconds the soft coloured clouds in your periphery will fade and vanish — leaving a blank field. Flick your eyes, and they snap back. (Move your mouse or tap to reset.)

Nothing on the screen actually disappears — the clouds are still there the whole time. What fades is your response to them. Neurons adapt to anything unchanging; a stimulus that never moves on the retina stops being news, and the visual system simply stops reporting it. Normally your eyes drift and jitter constantly (microsaccades), refreshing the image and keeping the world visible — but a soft, low-contrast, peripheral target held on a steady gaze defeats that, and it drops out of consciousness entirely. The blank you see is not on the screen; it is your visual system deciding the unchanging is not worth representing. Even the persistence of the world is something the brain has to keep choosing to construct.
