The dots that vanish · motion-induced blindness

Three bright yellow dots, impossible to miss. Stare at the red centre while the blue grid rotates, and — one at a time — the yellow dots will blink out of existence, sometimes for seconds, though they never actually leave. (Move the mouse or tap to reset.)

The yellow dots are drawn every single frame — measure the pixels and they are always there. Yet a moving global pattern can erase a vivid, high-contrast target from awareness, completely and repeatedly. Why a healthy visual system does this is still debated: the constantly moving surface may be read as a continuous surface that "ought" to occlude the dots, so the brain suppresses them; or the steady, unchanging dots simply lose the competition for representation against the salient motion. Either way the lesson is the same and it is stark — salience does not guarantee awareness. Something can be bright, present, and fixated, and your visual system can still decide not to show it to you. What you see is curated, not transcribed.
