A cell that watches one spot

One retinal ganglion cell. Its centre says "light here, fire"; its surround says "light here, hush". Drag the spotlight across the field and watch when the cell actually cares.

Firing rate

Drop a small spot dead-centre and the cell roars. Grow it until it spills into the surround and the firing collapses. Flood the field evenly — bright everywhere — and the cell goes nearly silent. The neuron does not report how much light there is; it reports differences in light across space. Before any "image" exists, the retina has already thrown away absolute brightness and kept only contrast. Seeing begins by discarding most of what the photons carry.
