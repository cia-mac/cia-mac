Two cubes in one drawing

Twelve lines, no depth information at all. Your visual system refuses to see it that way — it builds a cube, commits to one of two, and flips between them.

Click the drawing: the shaded face becomes the front, and the whole cube reorients. Same twelve lines each time.

Self-experiment — your flip rate

Leave it ambiguous, stare at the dot, and tap the button (or spacebar) every time the cube reverses on its own. Thirty seconds.

The retinal image is identical in every case — a flat line drawing. The depth, the orientation, the "front" face: none of it is in the stimulus. Your visual system constructs a 3D object from 2D evidence, and when the evidence supports two objects equally, it alternates between whole interpretations rather than settling on the flat truth. You cannot choose to see it as twelve flat lines — construction is mandatory.
